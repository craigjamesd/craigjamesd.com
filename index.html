About the Artist

CJ Davitt —
Angling Artist

CJ Davitt is a New Jersey painter whose work is inseparable from the salt water that has shaped nearly every chapter of his life.

Growing up, his family summered in Lavallette on the shores of Barnegat Bay. Those early years — fishing for snappers with bobber rigs, crabbing on the family's old Dixie, watching the light shift over the open water — became the visual vocabulary he would later pour onto canvas.

"I started painting bobbers because, when I was young, that's what we did at the shore. We fished for snappers with bobber rigs in the bay."

Today, CJ lives near Sandy Hook with his wife and two kids, just a short walk from the water. By day he works as a physician's assistant in orthopedic surgery — a demanding career that makes the studio all the more essential. Painting, like fishing, forces him to be completely present. It is his reset.

His transition from colored pencil illustration to oil painting began during the pandemic, when he had more time at home and a hunger for a new challenge. He taught himself to work with oil on canvas — navigating its slower dry times, its tendency to blend when you least want it to, its occasional happy accidents. A misplaced brushstroke once became an entire flock of seagulls. He kept them.

His technique places heavy emphasis on shadow, texture, and hyper-realism. He occasionally trades his brush for a palette knife, loading thick paint that dries with a sculptural surface — the bobbers in his paintings look like you could reach out and pick them up. His goal: that if someone sees a painting of his without a signature, they'd still know it was his.

"I just paint the things I like because they have meaning to me. If people enjoy them or find meaning in my work, that's great."
The Drifter, 30″ × 40″, oil on canvas

Weathered bobbers. Bushels of blue crabs. Stormy open water. Fishermen in yellow slickers. Rope knots. Striped bass. Shark teeth. Seaside shops. Every subject is a product of where CJ was raised and where he lives — the Jersey Shore, in all its battered, beautiful, salt-stained glory.

The Story

From Barnegat Bay to gallery walls

Early Years
Summers on Barnegat Bay
Growing up in New Jersey, CJ's family summered in Lavallette. Fishing for snappers with bobber rigs, crabbing off the dock, and spending long days on the family boat planted the seeds of a lifelong love of the coast.
Pre-pandemic
Colored Pencil & Logo Work
Like his father before him, CJ developed a talent for illustration — drawing designs for fishing buddies to use on hats and boat logos. His hyperrealist pencil work, focused on the minute detail of fish scales and tackle, built the foundation for everything to come.
2020
First Oil on Canvas
The pandemic gave him time and a hunger for challenge. He picked up a brush and started experimenting with oil paint in his Lavallette beach home studio. The learning curve was steep — oil stays wet longer, colors blend unexpectedly — but a happy accident involving a misplaced white streak became a flock of seagulls, and he was hooked.
2021–2023
Finding His Identity
The bobber paintings became CJ's signature — familiar, nostalgic objects rendered with stunning realism. He developed a style heavy on shadow, texture, and depth. The palette knife entered the process, enabling him to build thick, tactile surfaces that made his subjects pop off the canvas in a way no brush alone could achieve.
2024–2025
Gallery Recognition
Thanks to encouragement from his wife and quickly running out of wall space at home, CJ began placing work in New Jersey galleries — Frederick Gallery in Spring Lake, Autumn Elise Gallery in Rumson, and Noon Designs in Bay Head. The response from collectors was immediate.
2026
On The Water — Featured Angling Artist
CJ was selected as the featured Angling Artist in the 2026 On The Water Angler's Almanac — the Mid-Atlantic's premier fishing publication. The multi-page feature introduced his work to an entirely new audience of collectors who share his love of the coast.

"Over a short time, Davitt has developed a unique style that makes his work easy to identify even without his signature."

— On The Water Magazine, January 2026
